# OpenGate Sprint 3 Retrospective

**Document version:** 1.0
**Sprint:** Sprint 3 (Epic E2 identity foundations — sessions, authorization, tenant isolation)
**Sprint window:** 2026-06-02 to 2026-06-07 (LLM-assisted execution)
**Stories delivered:** US-02.03, US-02.04, US-02.05
**Story points committed / delivered:** 15 / 15
**Author:** OpenGate coordination team

## Purpose

This document is the backward-looking analytical record of Sprint 3. It complements the forward-looking Sprint 3 Handoff, which is the tight entry point for a fresh Claude session beginning Sprint 4. Where the Handoff is operational, this Retrospective is reflective: it captures velocity reality, story-level outcomes with nuance, the process changes that distinguished Sprint 3 from Sprint 2, the errors caught during the sprint and how they were caught, and recommendations for Sprint 4.

The register is engineer-to-engineer, as the project standard requires. Where something went well it is named; where something went poorly it is named just as plainly. Sprint 3 went poorly in more places than Sprint 2 did, and those are named first in the velocity and errors sections, because they are the most useful part of the record.

## Sprint goal recap

Sprint 3 completed the security spine of the identity foundation, the three stories that make multi-tenant isolation and authorization real. US-02.03 delivered the `sessions` table, the login/logout/refresh use case, and the session middleware, on the pre-authentication/post-authentication connection-pool split. US-02.04 delivered the Casbin authorization adapter, its policy-refreshing enforcer, and the per-route fail-closed enforcement middleware. US-02.05 enabled and forced Row-Level Security on the identity tables with the `tenant_isolation` policies and the dual-layer verification test.

Three stories aggregating to 15 story points (5 each, per Implementation Plan section 22 as corrected in v1.1) were committed against a stated sprint capacity of 9 — a 67 percent overcommit, the largest of the project so far, reflecting both that all three are five-point stories and that LLM-assisted execution sustains a cadence a solo developer could not. The sequence ran in dependency order: US-02.03 → US-02.04 → US-02.05, the order the Implementation Plan and the dependency matrix prescribe; the coordinator briefly considered and then rejected reordering US-02.05 ahead of US-02.04, confirming the plan order after checking that `casbin_rules` is global and carries no RLS interaction that would force the swap.

## Delivery summary

All three committed stories were delivered. Zero carry-over to Sprint 4. US-02.03 merged to `main` first (it is the dependency root of the other two), across twelve commits spanning its steps and three pre-merge corrections; US-02.04 merged across three commits (`cc5869b` the `casbin_rules` table and seed, `47bfd2c` the model and policy-refreshing authorizer with the bypass-only grant, `640f31b` the HTTP enforcement and hardening); US-02.05 is committed and verified at `8345e8f` with its pull request (#7) the final merge step. Every merge was rebase-merge to keep `main` linear; the commits preserve their Conventional Commits messages and the `feat(US-XX.XX):` story scope.

The most consequential difference from Sprint 2 is not in the delivery — both sprints delivered their committed scope cleanly with nothing broken merged — but in the amount of in-sprint iteration the delivery required, analyzed next.

## Velocity analysis

Planned capacity was 9 story points; delivered scope was 15. Cumulative delivery across the first three sprints is 37 story points (12 in Sprint 1, 10 in Sprint 2, 15 in Sprint 3).

The R-01 risk trigger — cumulative delivery below 21 story points across the first three sprints — is the formal Sprint-3-end re-evaluation point. At 37 against a 21 threshold, the trigger does not fire and R-01 is now formally cleared, not merely effectively cleared as at the Sprint 2 checkpoint. The standing caveat holds: this velocity reflects LLM-assisted execution with minutes-per-story human review, not a sustainable solo cadence, and must not be projected forward.

The honest velocity signal is that Sprint 3 did **not** repeat Sprint 2's zero-correction record, and by a wide margin. US-02.03 took three pre-merge corrections after its initial implementation; US-02.04 took two hardening amendments plus a placement refinement. This is a regression in single-pass delivery from Sprint 2, and it has two distinct causes that should not be conflated. The first cause is genuine: the security-critical surface of these stories — a forwarded-header trust model, RLS policy semantics, role privileges, a fail-closed authorization path — is exactly the kind of work where the first cut is rarely the last, and several of the iterations closed real defects (the client-IP spoof vector, a startup-panic path, two privilege gaps) rather than reworking cosmetics. The second cause is less flattering: a meaningful share of the iteration traced to coordinator prompt errors and gaps, enumerated in the errors section — the client-IP hop direction was specified backwards, the commit scope was specified wrong, and two database-privilege gaps shipped in prompts because the coordinator reasoned from the design documents instead of the realized database state. The executor caught most of these. The lesson is in the process-changes and dynamics sections: for database-state-dependent work, the prompt must drive empirical verification, because the documents do not show the realized grants and role attributes.

What did hold, and matters more than the single-pass count, is that no correction was a post-merge fix. Every error was caught before its story merged — by review, by the executor's preflight, or by the coordinator's own re-reading — so `main` never carried a defect. The iteration was front-loaded into the verify-then-next loop, which is where it belongs.

## Story-by-story outcomes

**US-02.03 — Sessions, login flow, and the connection-pool split (5 SP).** The heaviest story of the sprint and the one where the most deferred pieces landed: the regular RLS-bound pool with its tenant-binding hooks, `sqlc` introduced for the first tenant-scoped queries, a `sqlc`-drift CI job, the `sessions` and slug migrations, the authentication use cases, and the RFC 9457 Problem Details HTTP surface. The architectural spine is the pre-authentication/post-authentication pool split: a BYPASSRLS pool with explicit tenant arguments for the pre-auth identity lookups, and an RLS-bound pool with a panicking `FromContext` for the post-auth path. Locked decisions included one Argon2id verify per login with a uniform `ErrInvalidCredentials` for every credential failure (the user-enumeration timing defense deferred from US-02.02), the sliding idle window materialized in `expires_at`, mixed freshness (role snapshotted at issue, tenant status checked live), Problem Details bodies from a static mapping only, and the `__Host-` cookie prefix conditioned on `CookieSecure`.

It took three pre-merge corrections. The first was a genuine environment fix: a flaky Postgres testcontainer startup race (the initdb restart emits the readiness log twice) resolved with `wait.ForAll` plus `WithOccurrence(2)`, not retries or sleeps, and then consolidated into a shared `internal/testsupport` helper so the fix lands once for all six container sites. The second and third were security corrections to the coordinator's own client-IP handling: the hop was first specified as the leftmost X-Forwarded-For entry, which is client-controllable, and corrected to the rightmost hop; then hardened against a multi-line-header spoof by joining `Header.Values` rather than reading a single header line. Both corrections were the coordinator's to own. The realized scope also diverged from the plan: the pool and hooks landed here, not in US-02.05 as the v1.1 plan placed them, because the session middleware's authenticated path needs the pool — recorded in the v1.2 reconciliation.

**US-02.04 — Casbin authorization adapter and middleware (5 SP).** Three commits, three layers. The first is the `casbin_rules` table — a global table with no `tenant_id` and therefore no RLS — and the verbatim seventeen-row v1 policy seed from Database Schema §5.6, with a regression test that asserts the negative case (`auditor, members, write` absent) so an over-grant cannot pass silently. The second is the flat ACL-with-wildcard model embedded via `go:embed`, a custom `sqlc` policy loader on the bypass pool rather than a Casbin database-adapter library (to stay on pgx/sqlc), and a `CasbinAuthorizer` that caches behind an `atomic.Pointer`, refreshes by thirty-second polling, retains the last-good policy on a refresh-load error, and swaps to deny-all on a successful zero-row load. The third is the per-route `requirePermission` middleware with strict fail-closed semantics — an enforce error or a missing principal returns 500 and never runs the protected handler; a denial returns a generic 403 whose body never names the resource or action — wired into the `api` subcommand with a fail-fast initial load.

Two design judgments were signed off before the prompts: per-route middleware over a central path-map, and polling-only refresh with the LISTEN/NOTIFY fast-path deferred (the design documents framed policy refresh as LISTEN/NOTIFY-driven, which the migration-managed reality contradicts — recorded in the v1.2 reconciliation). The story also surfaced a sequencing correction worth keeping: the River queue and `cleanup.sessions`, which a stale handoff note had placed in Sprint 3, are in fact US-03.04 in Epic E3; the coordinator found this while labeling the story, presented the options, and the arbiter chose to follow the plan and defer River. The River design work done before the catch (the dedicated `river` schema, the `rivermigrate` integration, the v0.38.0 pins, all verified against primary sources) is preserved for US-03.04.

The two database-privilege catches in this story are the sprint's clearest process lesson. The step-one `casbin_rules` migration granted no runtime privileges at all — the table was readable in the test only because the test connected as the superuser, which masked the gap — and the executor caught it, leading to a bypass-only SELECT grant added as an append-only migration. The hardening at the end (a positive-interval guard so `time.NewTicker` cannot panic on a misconfigured `AUTHZ_REFRESH_INTERVAL`, and a deferred listener close) was folded in amendments, and the interval-check placement was then refined to validate before acquiring resources — a change whose test interaction the executor flagged (the bind-error test's config carried a zero interval that a top-of-function check would have tripped). The commit scope was initially written `feat(authz):` against the project's `feat(US-XX.XX):` convention and corrected, with the convention saved to memory.

**US-02.05 — RLS and tenant_isolation policies with dual-layer verification (5 SP).** A single commit, and the cleanest story of the sprint despite being the one where a false-green was easiest to ship. Because the pool and hooks landed in US-02.03 and `opengate_bypass` was created `WITH BYPASSRLS` in the role migration, the story reduced to the RLS enable-and-force migration on `tenants`, `users`, `sessions` and the dual-layer verification test. The policy form is the locked deviation from the literal Database Schema §13 / System Design §10 SQL: `nullif(current_setting('app.current_tenant_id', true), '')::uuid`, because the pool leaves the session variable empty or unset on a context-less checkout and the literal cast raises on that, where AC2 requires zero rows. The migration uses `FORCE` so the policy binds even the table owner, which matters because `tenants` is owned by the migration-runner role.

The story's value lay in the verification test actually exercising RLS, which it does only because the regular pool connects as `opengate_app` — a non-superuser, RLS-subject role — and not the testcontainer superuser, which would have bypassed RLS and produced a vacuous pass. The executor confirmed this empirically against the realized helpers before writing the test. It also caught a gap the coordinator's prompt had assumed away: `opengate_app` holds no SELECT on `tenants` or `users` in v1, because nothing reads them on the regular pool yet, so the test's AC1–AC3 queries would have failed with permission-denied rather than demonstrating row-filtering. The executor resolved it with a test-only superuser grant in the arrange phase — kept out of the migration, since a production grant would be dead privilege until a regular-pool reader exists — and the coordinator confirmed the least-privilege choice. The AC2 assertions were made non-vacuous by seeding rows in all three tables, including a session, so zero rows under no tenant context proves RLS rather than an empty table.

## Process changes from Sprint 2 to Sprint 3

Two disciplines were added in Sprint 3 and should carry into Sprint 4; the Sprint 2 disciplines all continued unchanged.

The first and most important is the executor's empirical preflight for database-state-dependent work. Sprint 3 repeatedly found that the design documents do not show the realized grants, role attributes, and connection roles, and that prompts reasoned from the documents alone shipped with privilege gaps. The countermeasure that emerged — and that caught the `casbin_rules` and `opengate_app` grant gaps and confirmed the US-02.05 pool roles — is to probe the actual database (`pg_roles`, `has_table_privilege`, how the existing tests connect) before writing a migration or a test that depends on that state. This is now a standing discipline: for RLS, grants, and role attributes, verify the realized state, do not infer it from the schema document.

The second is independent verification of library APIs, not just versions. Sprint 2 established `git ls-remote` spot-checks of dependency version claims; Sprint 3 extended this to API signatures, verifying the River v0.38.0 migration API and the Casbin v2.135.0 enforcer and nil-adapter behavior against the upstream source before writing prompts that depend on them. The `AddPolicies`-is-safe-with-a-nil-adapter check and the `rivermigrate.New(nil)` check are examples; both could have produced a broken prompt if assumed.

Two conventions were also pinned. The commit scope is `feat(US-XX.XX):` keyed to the story for story-line commits, with a component scope (`http`, `test`) reserved for cross-cutting commits — saved to memory after the `feat(authz):` slip. And the pull-request description was settled as a one-screen reviewer-orientation artifact — a summary, the review-critical decisions a reviewer could break, the AC-to-test map, a verify-locally block, and out-of-scope notes — after the first US-02.03 draft ran long; design rationale belongs in commit bodies and the reconciliation record, not the PR body.

## Decisions made during sprint that revise the Implementation Plan

The v1.2 reconciliation pass was run at sprint close and is the consolidated record of Sprint 3's planning-document revisions. Five items, all genuine Sprint-3 realization divergences that the v1.1 pass (written before this implementation) could not have anticipated; the pre-Sprint-3 backlog was closed by v1.1 and is not reopened.

The RLS policy form: Database Schema §13 and System Design §10 write the policy with the literal `current_setting('app.current_tenant_id')::uuid`, which raises on the empty or unset variable the pool produces with no tenant in context; the realized form is the null-safe `nullif(current_setting('app.current_tenant_id', true), '')::uuid`, applied uniformly across all §13 policies so the not-yet-built tables do not repeat the error. The pgx hook names: the §10 code block uses `AfterAcquire`/`BeforeRelease`, which are not fields of `pgxpool.Config` (the real fields are `BeforeAcquire`/`AfterRelease`, the latter taking no context), so the block as written does not compile; corrected, along with the bind-empty-and-warn behavior the realized hook adds. The Casbin policy refresh: §11 describes a user-management write path and a LISTEN/NOTIFY refresh signal, neither of which exists — the policy is migration-managed and refresh is polling-only. The realized pool scope: the pool and hooks landed in US-02.03, not US-02.05, so both stories carry realized-scope notes. The health endpoint: §21 names `/health/ready`, the realized path is `/readyz`.

No new runtime dependencies were added in Sprint 3 beyond what US-02.04's authorization work required at the design level; the River v0.38.0 and Casbin v2.135.0 pins were verified but River's integration is deferred to US-03.04.

## Coordinator and executor dynamics

The two-step pattern — substantive decisions discussed and signed off before any prompt is written — continued and remained the right shape; the US-02.05 decomposition, where the policy-form deviation and the test-role linchpin were settled before the prompt, is the clearest example of it preventing a broken or vacuous deliverable. But Sprint 3 leaned harder on the executor's agency than any prior sprint, and that is the dynamic worth recording.

The executor's catches this sprint were more numerous and more consequential than Sprint 2's, and they shared a root: they were found by empirically inspecting the realized state rather than by re-reading the prompt. The `casbin_rules` migration granting no runtime privileges, the `opengate_app` tables being unreadable by the regular pool, and the bind-error test's zero-interval config interaction were all invisible from the design documents and the prompt text; the executor surfaced them by probing the database and reading the existing tests. This validates the report-back protocol — resolved versions, adaptations with rationale, per-AC output, and now empirical preflight findings were present in every report — and it shifts a share of correctness assurance onto the executor that the coordinator should plan for, not lean on by accident.

The coordinator's errors and under-specifications are named plainly, because honest accounting runs both directions and the coordinator's share was larger this sprint than last. The client-IP hop was specified leftmost when it should have been rightmost. The commit scope was specified `feat(authz):` against the established convention. The step-one Casbin migration prompt omitted the runtime grants. The US-02.05 prompt assumed `opengate_app` could read tables it has no grant on. The interval-check placement created a test tension. Each was caught and corrected before merge — most by the executor, the commit scope by the arbiter — and none reached `main`, but the pattern is clear: the more security-critical and database-state-dependent the work, the more the coordinator's document-grounded prompts need the executor's empirical check as a backstop.

## Errors and how they were caught

Six items of note. None reached `main`; all were caught in the verify-then-next loop.

The client-IP hop direction (leftmost specified, rightmost correct, then hardened against multi-line-header spoofing) was a coordinator decision error, caught during the pre-merge review of US-02.03's trust model and corrected across the fix and a follow-on hardening.

The `casbin_rules` runtime-grant gap (the step-one migration granted no privileges; the superuser test connection masked it) was caught by the executor, which recognized that the request path needs the bypass role to hold SELECT and that step one had granted none. The correction was a bypass-only grant migration.

The `opengate_app` table-grant assumption (US-02.05's AC1–AC3 assumed the regular pool could read `tenants`/`users`, which it cannot in v1) was caught by the executor's `has_table_privilege` preflight probe, and resolved with a test-only scaffolding grant rather than a dead-privilege migration grant.

The interval-check placement tension (a top-of-function check would have tripped the bind-error test's zero-interval config) was caught by the executor, which is what made the validate-before-acquire placement land with a one-line realistic-interval change to that test rather than a broken test.

The commit-scope deviation (`feat(authz):` against `feat(US-XX.XX):`) was caught by the arbiter and corrected message-only, and the convention was saved to memory.

The River sequencing contradiction (a stale note placing River and `cleanup.sessions` in Sprint 3 when they are US-03.04 in Epic E3) was caught by the coordinator while labeling the story against the Implementation Plan, surfaced with options, and resolved by following the plan.

## Recommendations for Sprint 4

Continue the full cadence: the two-step decision-then-prompt pattern, the branch-plus-PR flow, the PR-green-before-merge gate, independent verification of dependency versions and now library APIs, the executor report-back protocol, and — promoted to a first-class discipline this sprint — the empirical database-state preflight for any work touching grants, role attributes, or RLS. The single highest-leverage change for Sprint 4 is to write the database-state-dependent prompts to instruct the executor to probe and report the realized state first, rather than asserting it; the two privilege gaps this sprint both came from asserting state the documents did not actually establish.

Apply the v1.2 reconciliation to the corpus. The changelog is written; the corpus documents should carry the corrections as the v1.1 set was applied, so a fresh Sprint-4 session reading the planning documents is not misled — the literal RLS policy form and the non-compiling pgx hook block in System Design §10 are the two a forward implementer would most directly trip on.

Sprint 4 opens Epic E3, the event store and projection infrastructure, with US-03.01 (the `events` and `projection_progress` tables), US-03.02 (the `EventStore` port and the domain event types), and US-03.03 (the `PostgresEventStore` adapter with concurrency control) — nine points, and the point at which event sourcing finally arrives for the domain aggregates after three sprints of state-stored identity work. The coordinator should expect US-03.03 to need the most careful decomposition: optimistic concurrency control on an append-only event store, with the per-aggregate sequence and the conflict-retry semantics, is subtle and is the story where a wrong decision is most expensive to unwind. The deferred River integration (US-03.04) and `cleanup.sessions` ride later in E3; the River design work from Sprint 3 is preserved for it. R-01 is formally cleared but the plan's next formal velocity checkpoint should still be observed.

## Closing note

Sprint 3 delivered the identity foundation's security spine: dual-layer tenant isolation from the connection-pool binding and forced RLS, a polling-refreshed Casbin authorizer with fail-closed per-route enforcement, and the session and login flow on the pre-auth/post-auth pool split. It cost more iteration than Sprint 2 — three pre-merge corrections on US-02.03, two amendments and a refinement on US-02.04 — and a fair share of that traced to coordinator prompt errors and to reasoning from the design documents where the realized database state was what mattered. The executor's empirical catches, now a named discipline, are what kept those errors out of `main`. The codebase at sprint close has forced RLS on the identity tables with dual-layer verification, a fail-closed authorization layer over a migration-managed policy, and a working session and login flow; the git history is linear and every story is its own reviewed, CI-gated set of commits. Sprint 4 begins the event store. The work continues.
