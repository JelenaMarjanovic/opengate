# OpenGate Sprint 4 Retrospective

**Document version:** 1.0
**Sprint:** Sprint 4 (Epic E3 opening — event store and projection infrastructure)
**Sprint window:** 2026-06-07 to 2026-06-09 (LLM-assisted execution)
**Stories delivered:** US-03.01, US-03.02, US-03.03
**Story points committed / delivered:** 9 / 9
**Author:** OpenGate coordination team

## Purpose

This is the backward-looking analytical record of Sprint 4. It complements the forward-looking Sprint 4 Handoff, which is the tight entry point for a fresh Claude session beginning Sprint 5. Where the Handoff is operational, this Retrospective is reflective: velocity reality, story-level outcomes with nuance, the process changes that distinguished Sprint 4 from Sprint 3, the errors caught during the sprint and how, and recommendations for Sprint 5.

The register is engineer-to-engineer. Where something went well it is named; where something went poorly it is named just as plainly. Sprint 4 was the cleanest sprint of the project in execution terms — zero pre-merge code corrections — but that headline hides a real signal: the coordinator's prompts carried several small tensions the executor had to resolve, and two corpus-and-file problems were caught only by the coordinator grepping the realized artifact rather than trusting it. Both are named below, because they are the most useful part of the record.

## Sprint goal recap

Sprint 4 opened Epic E3 and is the point at which event sourcing finally arrives for the domain aggregates, after three sprints of state-stored identity work. US-03.01 delivered the `events` and `projection_progress` tables, the `events_stream_position_seq` sequence, the three query indexes, forced Row-Level Security with the `tenant_isolation` policy on `events`, and a SQL-level RLS isolation test. US-03.02 delivered the `EventStore` outbound port, the `Event` envelope and `EventMetadata` domain types, and the `ErrConcurrencyConflict` / `ErrEventNotFound` sentinels. US-03.03 delivered the `PostgresEventStore` adapter with optimistic concurrency control, the `InMemoryEventStore` double, the shared contract suite both pass, and the `events` role grants.

Three stories aggregating to 9 story points (2 + 2 + 5, per Implementation Plan section 22) were committed against a stated sprint capacity of 9 — the first sprint committed at exactly capacity, after three consecutive overcommits. The point count is light relative to the project's demonstrated cadence, but the difficulty was concentrated, not distributed: US-03.03 alone carried the optimistic concurrency control, and the handoff correctly predicted it would need the most careful decomposition. The sequence ran in dependency order US-03.01 → US-03.02 → US-03.03, the order the plan and dependency matrix prescribe.

## Delivery summary

All three committed stories were delivered. Zero carry-over to Sprint 5. The sprint opened with a corpus-hygiene fix unrelated to any story — pull request #8 (`cc48c07`), correcting two stale inline dependency fields the v1.1 reconciliation had missed (discussed in the errors section) — and then ran the three stories: US-03.01 at `cf21e30` (PR #9), US-03.02 at `e95fefd` (PR #10), and US-03.03 across two commits, `d3acc65` (the events read queries and role grants) and `be10473` (the adapter, the in-memory double, and the contract suite), as PR #11. Every merge was rebase-merge to keep `main` linear; the commits preserve their Conventional Commits messages and the `feat(US-XX.XX):` story scope, with PR #8 a `docs:` commit.

The defining difference from Sprint 3 is the absence of in-sprint code iteration. Sprint 3 needed three pre-merge corrections on one story and two amendments on another; Sprint 4 needed none. No `git commit --amend`, no follow-on hardening prompt, no correction prompt. Each story's first implementation passed verification. What replaced the execution iteration is analyzed in the dynamics and errors sections: the friction moved upstream, into the coordinator's prompts and the corpus.

## Velocity analysis

Planned capacity was 9 story points; delivered scope was 9. Cumulative delivery across the first four sprints is 46 story points (12, 10, 15, 9). R-01 was formally cleared at the Sprint 3 checkpoint and is not re-evaluated here; the standing caveat holds unchanged — this cadence reflects LLM-assisted execution with minutes-per-story human review and must not be projected forward.

The single-pass delivery record was the project's best: all three stories merged on their first implementation with no pre-merge code correction, reversing Sprint 3's regression. Two things produced that. The first is that US-03.01 and US-03.02 had, on inspection, no open architectural decisions — the corpus had already locked the table schema (Database Schema §6.1/§6.2/§13), the port signatures (System Design §2), and the type shapes; the coordinator's articulation step confirmed this and resisted manufacturing decision points, so the prompts were faithful transcription with verification rather than design. The second is that US-03.03's two-step decomposition isolated the SQL surface from the Go implementation, and the contract suite was specified before the prompt, so the adapter and the double were verified against the same assertions from the first run.

The honest counter-signal is that the friction did not vanish, it relocated. It appears in two places the single-pass count does not show: the coordinator caught two problems by grepping the realized artifact rather than trusting it (a v1.1-escapee in the corpus and a wrong-file upload), and three of the coordinator's prompts carried literal-code imperfections the executor corrected at implementation time. None of the latter were correctness or security defects — unlike Sprint 3's privilege gaps and trust-model error — but they are the same shape of coordinator imperfection, caught downstream, and they are cheap to prevent. That is the lesson, developed below.

## Story-by-story outcomes

**US-03.01 — events and projection_progress tables with RLS (2 SP).** One prompt, one commit, no correction. The story had no open architectural decisions: the coordinator's analysis found that what looked like three open questions — the per-aggregate `sequence` plus global `stream_position`, the `events` RLS behavior, and whether `projection_progress` carries RLS — were all already locked in Database Schema §6.1, §6.2, and §13. The work was faithful transcription with the precision points the prompt called out: `stream_position` is a bare column populated by the application, not a sequence default; the RLS migration is the `events`-only subset of §13 (reproducing the whole block would fail on tables that do not exist yet); `projection_progress` is deployment-global with no RLS. The null-safe policy form from the v1.2-reconciled §13 was transcribed verbatim. The one adaptation was outside the schema scope and not the coordinator's: the executor retargeted the existing `TestRLSMigrationRollback` from a blind one-step `Down` — which now reverted this story's `events` policy instead of the identity policies it asserts on — to a fixed-version `DownTo`, robust to stacked migrations, flagged it, and disclosed it (the coordinator recommended folding it into the commit body for git-log completeness, and it is in the PR description). The migrations-follow-code question was examined and found not to apply: the `events` RLS policy depends on the RLS-bound pool, which already existed from US-02.03/02.05, and the table-before-adapter order is the necessary `sqlc` order.

**US-03.02 — EventStore port and domain event types (2 SP).** One prompt, one commit, pure Go, no migrations. Three decisions were signed off before the prompt: the envelope JSON is snake_case aligned with the `events` columns with no `omitempty` (the field `Type` mapping to `event_type`); the `EventMetadata` shape is `CorrelationID` as a string aligned with the existing request-id correlation, a two-field actor (`ActorID` plus an extensible `ActorType`), OTel `TraceID`/`SpanID` hex strings populated best-effort later, and an app-version string, with no `CausationID` since §2 specifies correlation only; and the sentinels live in `internal/domain/events`. The one adaptation was the coordinator's error to own: the prompt's illustrative sentinel code block used a `events:` message prefix while the prose directed matching the prefix-less `auth`/`apperr` sentinels — a contradiction. The executor followed the explicit prose rule over the snippet, dropped the prefix for codebase consistency, and flagged it. AC1 (no circular dependency) was demonstrated as a build-and-layering guarantee via `go list`, the port's consumers not yet existing; AC2 (stable JSON) by a key-set test and a round-trip test using a fixed UTC time and a compact payload; AC3 by `errors.Is` on wrapped sentinels.

**US-03.03 — PostgresEventStore, in-memory double, contract suite (5 SP).** The hardest story, decomposed into two steps, both clean. Four decisions were signed off before the prompts: Model B, where `Append` assigns `sequence` from `expectedSequence` rather than the caller setting it (the cleaner single-source-of-truth, which also corrected the US-03.02 `Sequence` doc comment); Option 1, a single `INSERT` with `nextval()` inline and `RETURNING`, over a separate pre-read; a pool-agnostic adapter, `NewEventStore(db.DBTX)`, leaving the RLS-bound-versus-bypass pool choice to the composition root; and the role grants split — `opengate_app` with `SELECT`/`INSERT` plus the essential sequence `USAGE`, `opengate_bypass` with `SELECT` only. The constraint-specific 23505 mapping was specified as a correctness requirement, not a decision: only `events_aggregate_sequence_unique` is a concurrency conflict, while a 23505 on `events_stream_position_unique` or `events_pkey` surfaces as an internal error so a real defect is not masked — and the executor pinned all four cases with a white-box `TestMapAppendError`. Step 1 added the three sqlc read queries and the grants; its one deviation, named arguments on the time-range query, was an improvement on the coordinator's positional SQL, which would have emitted an `OccurredAt`/`OccurredAt_2` field pair. Step 2 added the adapter, the double, and the contract suite, with two adaptations that were coordinator-prompt imperfections: the literal `InMemoryEventStore` name the prompt specified would have stuttered under revive (`inmemory.InMemoryEventStore`) against the prompt's own zero-lint requirement, so the executor used the idiomatic `inmemory.EventStore`; and the `int → int32` `LIMIT` cast the prompt specified tripped gosec G115, which the executor resolved with a bounds clamp plus a justified `//nolint` matching repo precedent. The contract's adapter-agnostic assertions held: `Sequence` exactly, `StreamPosition` only relatively, because the shared Postgres sequence and the in-memory counter do not share an origin.

## Process changes from Sprint 3 to Sprint 4

The Sprint 3 disciplines all continued. The empirical database-state preflight was applied and validated again: US-03.01's RLS test confirmed it exercises real row-filtering only because the pool connects as the non-superuser `opengate_app`; US-03.03's grants were derived from the realized role model rather than asserted; and the executor verified empirically that an own-tenant `events` insert needs no `tenants` grant because foreign-key checks bypass RLS, keeping the contract arrange minimal. Independent verification, the two-step decision-then-prompt pattern, the branch-plus-PR flow with the green-before-merge gate, and the one-screen PR description all held.

One discipline is worth promoting to first-class, because it earned its place twice this sprint: the coordinator verifying the realized artifact by grepping it, not trusting it. The Sprint 3 lesson was that the executor must probe the realized database state; the Sprint 4 extension is that the coordinator must probe the realized corpus and the realized files. The coordinator caught the v1.1-escapee dependency fields by grepping the plan at sprint start, and caught a wrong-file upload — an asterisk-formatted, pre-fix copy of the plan that still carried the stale fields — by grepping the uploaded artifact before it replaced the project's good copy. Both were invisible without the grep; both would have propagated a stale or contradictory corpus into Sprint 5.

The two-step decomposition of the one hard story was the right call and is worth repeating for any five-point story: splitting US-03.03 into the SQL surface (queries plus grants) and the Go implementation (adapter, double, contract) gave each step an independent verification gate and kept the `Append`-versus-sqlc latitude where it belonged, with the implementation.

## Decisions made during sprint that revise the Implementation Plan

The corpus-hygiene fix in PR #8 is not a new decision but the completion of one the v1.1 reconciliation had recorded and only partly applied: v1.1 redirected US-03.01 and US-04.01 from the absorbed US-02.06 to the merged US-02.05 in the dependency matrix (section 24) but left the inline `_Dependencies:_` fields in the epic catalog pointing at US-02.06. The matrix was correct, its source was stale, and the two disagreed silently from v1.1 until this fix. It is recorded here and corrected on `main`; it is not a v1.3 item.

Two realization notes reached the v1.3 reconciliation decision at sprint close, where Jefi chose a minimal pass. The first is the §6.1 stream-position wording: §6.1 — an authoritative document — described the application reading `nextval` before the insert, whereas the realized `Append` obtains positions via `nextval()` inline and `RETURNING` within the single statement, which satisfies the "not a column default" rule and is the seam where a future change-notification path will read positions. This was first recorded as a note in the US-03.03 PR description in lieu of a formal corpus change; at sprint close it was upgraded to a formal §6.1 fold, because §6.1 is authoritative and the "read before insert" mechanism would mislead a forward implementer. The second is the Model B sequence assignment: System Design §2's illustrative `Event` struct presented `Sequence` as a field, and the realized contract is that `Append` assigns it from `expectedSequence`; the US-03.02 `Sequence` doc comment was corrected to match, and because §2 is explicitly illustrative v1.3 records the realization without a §2 edit. The `events` role grants required no schema-document change — the Database Schema has no role-grants section, so grants live only in the migrations. The §6.1 correction was applied directly on `main` (the Database Schema is now at v1.3), not through a pull request; the v1.3 changelog is the authoritative record of the delta, and the rest of the corpus is consistent with the delivered code.

## Coordinator and executor dynamics

The two-step pattern continued to be the right shape, and the locked-decisions-before-prompt discipline is what made the substance of every prompt implementable on the first pass. But Sprint 4 is the clearest illustration yet that the coordinator's prompts and the executor's judgment are a paired system, and that the executor carries a real share of correctness and quality assurance.

The executor's catches this sprint were less consequential than Sprint 3's — none touched correctness or security — but they were no less real, and honest accounting names the coordinator's share plainly: the sentinel-message prefix was a self-contradiction between the prompt's code block and its prose; the `InMemoryEventStore` name the prompt specified would have failed the lint gate the same prompt required; the `int → int32` cast the prompt specified tripped gosec; and the positional time-range parameters the prompt wrote would have produced an ugly generated field pair. Four prompt imperfections in one sprint, all in literal code the coordinator wrote into prompts, all caught and resolved by the executor with rationale, and three of them resolvable by a quick check of the project's lint rules and naming idioms before sending. The executor also added value beyond the literal prompt — the white-box `TestMapAppendError` covering every 23505 case, the gosec clamp as a genuine safety improvement rather than a blind suppression, the named-argument clarity on the time-range query. The report-back protocol carried all of this: every adaptation arrived with its reasoning and a revert path.

## Errors and how they were caught

Six items of note, of two distinct kinds. None reached `main`.

Two were corpus-and-file problems caught by the coordinator grepping the realized artifact. The v1.1-escapee dependency fields — US-03.01 and US-04.01 still pointing at the absorbed US-02.06 in their inline fields while the matrix was correct — were caught by grepping the plan before starting the sprint and fixed in PR #8. The wrong-file upload — an asterisk-formatted, pre-fix copy of the plan offered as the corrected one — was caught by grepping the uploaded file, which still showed the stale fields and a different emphasis style and line numbering than the merged version, and was resolved by re-export before it replaced the project's good copy.

Four were coordinator-prompt imperfections caught by the executor. The `events:` sentinel-message prefix contradicted the prompt's own prose directive to match the prefix-less convention, caught and resolved prefix-less. The literal `InMemoryEventStore` name would have stuttered under revive against the prompt's zero-lint requirement, caught and resolved as `inmemory.EventStore`. The `int → int32` `LIMIT` cast tripped gosec G115, caught and resolved with a bounded clamp and a justified suppression. The positional time-range parameters would have generated an `OccurredAt_2` field, caught and improved with named arguments.

## Recommendations for Sprint 5

Continue the full cadence: the two-step decision-then-prompt pattern, the branch-plus-PR flow, the green-before-merge gate, independent verification of dependency versions and library APIs, the empirical database-state preflight, the executor report-back protocol, and — promoted this sprint — the coordinator's grep-verification of the realized corpus and uploaded files rather than trusting them.

The one new discipline worth adding is small and cheap: when a prompt contains literal Go identifiers or code blocks, sanity-check them against the project's lint configuration and naming idioms before sending — the no-stutter rule, gosec's narrowing checks, and the established sentinel-message and parameter conventions. Three of this sprint's four prompt imperfections were literal-code details a coordinator-side idiom-and-lint pass would have caught; the executor's backstop worked, but prevention is cheaper than a flagged adaptation, and it keeps the prompt's authority intact.

The v1.3 reconciliation was settled at sprint close as a minimal pass: the §6.1 stream-position wording was folded into the corpus formally, and the Model B sequence assignment recorded in the v1.3 changelog without a System Design §2 edit, since §2 is illustrative. The §6.1 correction was applied directly on `main` rather than through a pull request, so the Database Schema is now at v1.3; the refreshed Sprint 5 handoff states the corpus version accordingly.

Sprint 5 continues Epic E3. The deferred River queue integration and `cleanup.sessions` (US-03.04), preserved with their verified v0.38.0 design work since Sprint 3, ride here, as do the projection and projector stories that consume the event store through `ReadAfterPosition`, write `projection_progress`, and require the `projection_progress` grants and the bypass-pool wiring of the `EventStore` that this sprint deliberately deferred. The exact Sprint 5 commitment follows Implementation Plan section 22. The coordinator should expect the first projector story to surface the bypass-pool construction of `PostgresEventStore` and the deferred `projection_progress` grants as its setup; the pool-agnostic adapter built this sprint is what makes that wiring a composition-root concern rather than an adapter change. The plan's next formal velocity checkpoint should be observed.

## Closing note

Sprint 4 delivered the event store: an append-only `events` table with forced RLS and optimistic concurrency control through the per-aggregate sequence constraint, a clean `EventStore` port with a typed event envelope, and the `PostgresEventStore` adapter and `InMemoryEventStore` double behind a single contract suite — the point at which event sourcing arrives for the domain after three sprints of state-stored identity work. It was the project's cleanest sprint in execution, with no pre-merge code correction on any story, and the credit for that belongs to the corpus having locked the early stories' design and to the two-step decomposition of the hard one. The honest counter-record is that the friction moved upstream: the coordinator's prompts carried four literal-code imperfections the executor resolved, and two corpus-and-file problems were caught only by the coordinator grepping the realized artifact. None reached `main`. The codebase at sprint close has the event store with concurrency control, two adapters under a shared contract, RLS on `events`, and the command-path grants; the git history is linear and every story is its own reviewed, CI-gated set of commits. Sprint 5 continues the event infrastructure. The work continues.
