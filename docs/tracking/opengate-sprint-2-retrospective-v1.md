**Sprint window:** 2026-05-30 to 2026-05-31 (LLM-assisted execution)

## Purpose

This document is the backward-looking analytical record of Sprint 2. It complements the forward-looking Sprint 2 Handoff, which is the tight entry point for a fresh Claude session beginning Sprint 3. Where the Handoff is operational, this Retrospective is reflective: it captures velocity reality, story-level outcomes with nuance, the process changes that distinguished Sprint 2 from Sprint 1, the errors caught during the sprint and how they were caught, and recommendations for Sprint 3.

The register is engineer-to-engineer, as the project standard requires. Where something went well it is named; where something went poorly it is named just as plainly.

## Sprint goal recap

Sprint 2 had two halves. The first closed out Epic E1 (foundations): US-01.05 added the GitHub Actions CI pipeline, and US-01.06 established the structured-logging and error-wrapping conventions for the whole codebase. The second opened Epic E2 (identity): US-02.01 delivered the tenant and user aggregates and the bootstrap CLI subcommand, and US-02.02 completed the Argon2id password module with verification.

Four stories aggregating to 10 story points (US-01.05 at 3, US-01.06 at 2, US-02.01 at 3, US-02.02 at 2, per Implementation Plan section 22) were committed against a stated sprint capacity of 9 — an 11 percent overcommit, materially milder than Sprint 1's 33 percent. The sequence was run in dependency order: US-01.05 → US-01.06 → US-02.01 → US-02.02. The arbiter explicitly chose to run the full E1 tail (including US-01.05) rather than defer the CI story, after the coordinator surfaced that US-01.05 was deferrable at low risk.

## Delivery summary

All four committed stories were delivered. Zero carry-over to Sprint 3. The `main` branch advanced through four fast-forward merges — US-01.05 at `742607f`, US-01.06 at `dfaa83e`, US-02.01 at `9021f23`, US-02.02 at `e242108` — bringing the total history to nine commits (the planning corpus plus eight story commits). The history is linear; every story is one Conventional Commits-formatted commit with its narrative body, message preserved through rebase merge.

The most consequential process change from Sprint 1 is that Sprint 2 abandoned direct-to-`main` commits in favor of a branch-plus-pull-request flow with CI gating, made possible by US-01.05 landing the pipeline first. Every story after US-01.05 was developed on a feature branch, opened as a PR, verified by a clean-room CI run, and merged only after that run passed. This is detailed in the process-changes section.

## Velocity analysis

Planned capacity was 9 story points; delivered scope was 10. Cumulative delivery across the first two sprints is 22 story points (12 in Sprint 1, 10 in Sprint 2).

The R-01 risk trigger fires if cumulative delivery across the first three sprints falls below 21 story points. Two sprints in, the cumulative already stands at 22, so the trigger cannot fire at the Sprint 3 checkpoint regardless of Sprint 3's outcome; the trigger is effectively cleared, though the formal re-evaluation point remains the end of Sprint 3. The same caveat from Sprint 1 applies: this velocity reflects LLM-assisted execution with minutes-per-story human review, not a sustainable solo-developer cadence, and must not be projected forward as a baseline.

One velocity signal improved over Sprint 1 and is worth stating precisely: Sprint 2 required **zero correction prompts**. Every story was completed in a single executor pass. Sprint 1 had two micro-correction cycles (US-01.03 re-verification, US-01.04 action-validation fix); Sprint 2 had none. The executor's deviations in Sprint 2 were all correct adaptations forced by the environment or the linter, not errors requiring rework. This is partly attributable to the prompts carrying more reference code than Sprint 1's, and partly to the design ambiguities being resolved before the prompts were written (see US-02.01).

## Story-by-story outcomes

**US-01.05 — GitHub Actions CI workflow (3 SP).** Delivered single-shot. Five substantive decisions were locked before the prompt. The pipeline runs the existing `make ci` so there is one source of truth for build, lint, and test, with no CI-versus-local drift (D2). Postgres for integration tests comes from testcontainers via the runner's Docker daemon, not a `services:` block, which would sit unused since the test code spins its own container (D1). Coverage is emitted as a downloadable artifact with the total surfaced in the run log, with no third-party coverage service (D3). Triggers are `push` to `main` plus every `pull_request`, with a concurrency group that cancels superseded runs (D4). Security posture is least-privilege `permissions: contents: read`, no `pull_request_target`, and all actions pinned to full commit SHA (D5).

One Sprint-1 file was touched: the Makefile `test` target gained `-coverprofile=coverage.out -covermode=atomic` so coverage falls out of the single test run rather than a wasteful second run. This was surfaced explicitly as a conscious edit rather than slipped in.

Three executor adaptations were reported. The action major versions were pinned at `actions/checkout@v6.0.2`, `actions/setup-go@v6.4.0`, and `actions/upload-artifact@v7.0.1` — newer than the stale `v4/v5/v4` parentheticals in the prompt, correctly following the prompt's explicit "resolve the latest stable release" instruction over the comment. The `.gitignore` was left untouched because both coverage artifacts were already ignored (one by an explicit line, one by a `*.out` glob). The separate tooling-install step was removed because `make ci` already lists `tools` as its first prerequisite. The coordinator independently verified all three pinned action SHAs against their tags via `git ls-remote` — all matched. The coordinator also disclosed dropping a `./bin` linter cache from the prompt's "mechanical defaults": its benefit depended on whether `make tools` is install-guarded (not visible), the install is cheap, and `setup-go`'s cache already covers the expensive module and build caches.

A non-obvious detail surfaced during AC2 (deliberate failing test). Because the push trigger is scoped to `main` only (D4), a push to an arbitrary feature branch does not run CI; the only way to exercise the workflow on a branch is through its open PR. The AC2 red-path demonstration therefore had to ride the existing PR branch, not a standalone branch. The failure surfaced as exit code 2, not 1: `go test` exits 1 on a test failure, but GNU `make` returns its own status 2 when a recipe fails, and that is the process exit the step reports. The failing test name appeared in the step log as expected, which is the agreed AC2 bar; literal per-test inline annotations remain deferred.

**US-01.06 — Structured logging and error-wrapping conventions (2 SP).** Delivered single-shot. Four decisions were locked. Logger delivery is hybrid: a configured `*slog.Logger` is injected as the primary mechanism and also set as the slog default as a safety net for stray library logs (D-A). Context enrichment is done by a custom `slog.Handler` that reads `trace_id`/`span_id` from the active OpenTelemetry span context and `tenant_id` from the tenant context, rather than per-call attributes (D-B). Sensitive-key redaction is deny-list, not allow-list (D-C). Two new packages were introduced, `internal/observability` and `internal/tenant` (D-D), alongside two more the story implied: `internal/config` (minimal, carrying only `LogLevel`, per the config-follows-code discipline) and `internal/apperr` (the generic `ErrInternal` sentinel, created slightly ahead of its first consumer but justified by AC2 needing a concrete sentinel to traverse).

`tenant.ID` was introduced string-backed with an explicit note that US-02.01 might promote it to UUID-backed — which it then did.

Two new dependencies were added: `kelseyhightower/envconfig` (chosen for its tag syntax matching the System Design section 23 example) and the OpenTelemetry trace API (the API only, not the SDK). The envconfig library was verified to parse `slog.Level` natively via `encoding.TextUnmarshaler`, so no custom decoder was needed.

The OpenTelemetry trace pin is the sprint's clearest lesson. The executor first pinned `v1.43.0` and reported it as "latest stable," but the coordinator's `git ls-remote` spot-check found the latest tag was `v1.44.0`; Go's minimum-version selection had retained the version already in the dependency graph rather than upgrading. The bump to `v1.44.0` was applied and re-verified clean. The takeaway, now a standing discipline: dependency "latest" claims are verified independently, not trusted.

**US-02.01 — Tenant aggregate and bootstrap CLI subcommand (3 SP).** Delivered single-shot, but only after a documentation contradiction was resolved before the prompt was written — the highest-value coordination work of the sprint. System Design section 11 frames the tenant and user aggregates as having events (event-sourced language), while the story's acceptance criteria say "insert a tenant row and a user row," and the event store does not exist (it is Epic E3, and US-02.01 depends only on US-01.04). Database Schema section 5 is explicit where section 11 is ambiguous: the identity tables are *not* event-sourced; they are authoritative state mutated by ordinary inserts and updates. Section 5 was treated as the authority, resolving the aggregates to state-stored. This is a case study in the corpus's internal tension being resolved by the more specific document rather than by guessing.

Four decisions followed. The password module was split along hash/verify, with US-02.01 delivering `HashPassword` and US-02.02 deferred for `VerifyPassword` (D1, the arbiter's choice of option B over reordering). US-02.01 builds only the BYPASSRLS connection pool; the regular RLS-bound pool and its hooks are deferred to US-02.03 (D2). `tenant.ID` was promoted to UUID-backed, since the RLS policy casts to `::uuid` and the event envelope uses `uuid.UUID` (D3). The persistence port is a single atomic `CreateTenantWithOwner` whose adapter runs both inserts in one transaction (D4).

A migration-state recon before the prompt found that the existing first migration creates the full `tenants` table (byte-for-byte the section 5.1 DDL), not the application roles. This confirmed a Sprint 1 deviation: US-01.04's plan and section 13 imply the first migration creates `opengate_app` and `opengate_bypass`, but the actual first migration created the `tenants` table instead (a smoke-test target for the goose round-trip; a role-only migration is awkward to round-trip). The dropped roles requirement was recovered in US-02.01, which adds the roles migration. A further gap surfaced: section 13 grants only `USAGE ON SCHEMA` to the roles, no table privileges, so the bootstrap (running as `opengate_bypass`) would have no rights on the tables it writes; US-02.01 adds `GRANT SELECT, INSERT` on `tenants` and `users` to fill the gap. The role connects via the default `PUBLIC` `CONNECT` grant, which works in dev and test but would fail in a hardened production database that revokes `PUBLIC` `CONNECT` — a design-level item for the v1.1 pass. Because section 5.1 has no unique constraint on tenant name (by design — two gyms may share a name), AC3's duplicate detection is a name pre-check inside the bootstrap transaction rather than a schema constraint.

Two dependencies were added and both verified latest by spot-check: `google/uuid v1.6.0` and `golang.org/x/crypto v0.52.0`. Executor adaptations were uniformly sound. The migration round-trip test was changed from `Down()` to `DownTo(ctx, 0)` because with three migrations a single `Down` reverts only the newest, never exercising the roles and tenants down sections — a sharp catch that prevented a falsely green round-trip. The `tenant.ID` UUID promotion broke not only `logger_test.go` (which the prompt named) but also `tenant_test.go` (which the prompt did not name); the executor correctly identified the second as falling under "the tenant.ID type change" — a coordinator under-specification, caught by the executor. A `gosec` G101 finding on an environment-variable name (not a credential) was handled with a documented local `//nolint` rather than a global exclusion, keeping the rule active elsewhere. The `time.Duration`-to-`interval` mapping was empirically verified by scanning the column back and comparing, rather than assumed.

Three coordinator implementation calls were flagged in the prompt for the arbiter to veto: raw `pgx` rather than introducing `sqlc` (the bootstrap is not a tenant-scoped query, which is `sqlc`'s purpose); `BypassRLSURL` optional in config and validated by the subcommand rather than required globally (a required field would break the unrelated `migrate` subcommand); and app-generated UUIDv7 identifiers produced in the application layer to keep domain constructors pure.

**US-02.02 — Password verification with rehash detection (2 SP).** Delivered single-shot. AC1 (the PHC hash format) was already satisfied by US-02.01's `HashPassword`, a consequence of D1, so this story was `VerifyPassword` plus the `ErrPasswordMismatch` sentinel only. Four decisions: a malformed or unsupported stored hash returns an error wrapping `apperr.ErrInternal`, distinct from `ErrPasswordMismatch`, so the future login flow can route a wrong password to 401 and corrupt data to 500 (D-A, the coordinator's recommendation to reuse the generic sentinel rather than introduce a separate `ErrInvalidHash`); verification recomputes against the parameters parsed from the stored hash, not the current defaults, so older-parameter hashes still verify (D-B); the rehash flag is true when the stored cost parameters differ from the current defaults (D-C); `ErrPasswordMismatch` lives in `internal/auth` as a sentinel (D-D).

Two security guards were built in beyond the literal acceptance criteria, on the basis of the project's "check every hidden vulnerability" standard. An empty salt or hash is rejected as malformed rather than allowed to verify — without this guard, an empty-hash PHC would produce a zero-length candidate that compares equal to a zero-length stored hash, an authentication bypass. Degenerate cost parameters (`m`, `t`, or `p` of zero) are rejected before reaching `argon2.IDKey`. The comparison uses `crypto/subtle.ConstantTimeCompare` rather than `bytes.Equal`. The empty-hash case is in the malformed test table and passes, proving the bypass guard.

Two executor adaptations, both linter-forced and both sound. The parser was split into `parsePHC`, `parsePHCBody`, and a shared `failPHC` to satisfy `gocyclo`. The `uint32(len(hash))` conversion that `gosec` G115 flags was handled by adding a real `len(hash) > math.MaxUint32` bound guard plus a justified local `//nolint` — better than a bare suppression, because the suppression is now backed by an actual invariant. A latent property was noted but not acted on: the version check rejects anything but `v=19`, so a future change to `argon2.Version` would require version-upcasting rather than rejection; this is a remote consideration since the Argon2 version field has been stable for a decade.

## Process changes from Sprint 1 to Sprint 2

Three disciplines were introduced in Sprint 2 and should carry into Sprint 3.

The first is the branch-plus-PR workflow. Sprint 1 committed directly to `main`. From US-01.05 onward, every story is developed on a feature branch, opened as a PR with `gh pr create --fill`, and merged with `gh pr merge --rebase --delete-branch` to keep history linear and preserve the commit message. This produces a stronger portfolio signal (PRs gated by CI) and keeps `main` clean. The PR also makes the `pull_request` CI trigger meaningful, which it would not be under direct-to-`main`.

The second is the PR-green-before-merge gate: a PR is merged only after its CI run passes. This was observed for US-01.05, US-02.01, and US-02.02. It lapsed once, on US-01.06, where the PR was merged before the run was confirmed; the post-merge `main` run happened to be green, so no harm materialized, and for in-memory logic already verified locally the clean-room failure probability was low. The gate exists for the cases where it would not be harmless, and US-02.01 — the first story with real database migrations and a `CREATE ROLE` path — is exactly such a case; the gate was reinstated there and held. The lesson is that the gate matters most precisely where a clean-room failure is most plausible.

The third is independent verification of dependency claims. After the OpenTelemetry `v1.43.0`-versus-`v1.44.0` discrepancy, the coordinator adopted a standing `git ls-remote` spot-check of every "latest" dependency or action claim, and full-SHA verification of pinned GitHub Actions. This caught nothing further in the sprint (the `google/uuid` and `x/crypto` claims were correct), but the discipline is cheap and the one catch justified it.

## Decisions made during sprint that revise the Implementation Plan

The v1.1 reconciliation list, carried from Sprint 1 and grown through Sprint 2, is consolidated here as the single source of truth for the planning-document revision pass. That pass was scheduled for the gap between Sprint 2 and Sprint 3 and is now due.

Carried from Sprint 1 and still open: the narrative story/point counts (61 stories / 196 points in prose versus the authoritative 55 stories / 201 points in the table); the module path correction from `github.com/jefimarjanovic/opengate` to `github.com/JelenaMarjanovic/opengate`; and the Sprint 1 tooling and image-pin items already enumerated in the Sprint 1 retrospective (lefthook substitution, golangci-lint install pattern, host port remap, Grafana/Tempo/Caddy pins, US-01.04 migration-mechanism-only scope).

New in Sprint 2. The Go version: the PRD specifies "1.23 or higher, most recent stable as of project start," but the actual `go.mod` declares `go 1.26` and CI reads the version from `go-version-file: go.mod`, making the realized version the single source of truth; the PRD reference should be updated. The logging redaction: System Design section 8 describes an allow-list of permitted fields, but the implemented and design-intended behavior is a deny-list of sensitive keys; the section should be corrected to deny-list. The role and grant model: section 13 grants only `USAGE ON SCHEMA` and should additionally specify the table-level grants the bypass role requires (`SELECT, INSERT` on `tenants` and `users`, added in US-02.01), an explicit `CONNECT` grant so the model survives a hardened production database that revokes `PUBLIC` `CONNECT`, and a resolution of the ownership question (the `tenants` table is owned by the default role, not `opengate_app`, because it was created before the roles existed). The US-01.04 reality: the first migration created the `tenants` table, not the application roles that section 13 and the US-01.04 plan implied; the roles were recovered in US-02.01, and the plan should reflect the actual ordering. The US-02.02 scope: `HashPassword` was delivered in US-02.01 per decision D1, so US-02.02's AC1 was pre-satisfied and the story reduced to verification; the story descriptions and the point split should note that US-02.01 absorbed the hashing half.

The dependency pins added in Sprint 2, for the record: `github.com/kelseyhightower/envconfig v1.4.0`, `go.opentelemetry.io/otel`/`otel/metric`/`otel/trace v1.44.0`, `github.com/google/uuid v1.6.0`, and `golang.org/x/crypto v0.52.0`.

## Coordinator and executor dynamics

The two-step pattern from Sprint 1 — substantive decisions discussed and signed off before any prompt is written — continued and paid off most visibly on US-02.01, where resolving the event-sourcing contradiction and the migration-state ambiguity before writing the prompt is what made a complex three-point story a single-pass delivery. The pattern's cost is round-trips; its return is the absence of rework. Sprint 2's zero correction prompts is the evidence.

The coordinator's habit of reading the specific design sections on demand (sections 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 13, and Database Schema sections 5.1, 5.2 across the sprint) rather than preloading the corpus kept each decision grounded in the authoritative text and surfaced the contradictions that needed resolving. The migration-state recon before US-02.01 — inspecting the actual migration files rather than trusting the handoff's summary — is the model: it found both that the `tenants` table already existed and that the roles did not, either of which would have produced a broken prompt.

Executor agency was again a positive. The `DownTo(0)` round-trip fix, the recognition that `tenant_test.go` also needed the type change, the local-`//nolint` discipline on both `gosec` findings, and the empirical verification of the `pgx` interval mapping were all unprompted and correct. The report-back protocol held: resolved versions, adaptations with rationale, and per-AC output were present in every report, which is what let the arbiter validate without re-reading the codebase.

One coordinator under-specification is worth recording: the US-02.01 prompt named only `logger_test.go` as needing the `tenant.ID` type-change fix, missing `tenant_test.go`. The executor caught it. The lesson is that when a type's signature changes, the prompt should instruct the executor to find all references rather than enumerate them, since enumeration can be incomplete.

## Errors and how they were caught

Four items of note, none of which required discarding executor work.

The OpenTelemetry version drift (`v1.43.0` reported as latest when `v1.44.0` existed) was caught by the coordinator's `git ls-remote` spot-check before merge and corrected by an explicit bump. It was not an error so much as a stale claim that minimum-version selection produced silently; the spot-check is now standing practice.

The US-01.06 gate lapse (merging before the PR run was confirmed green) was caught after the fact by the post-merge `main` run, which was green. No harm materialized, but the deviation from the agreed gate was named openly and the gate was reinstated for the remaining stories.

The System Design section 11 versus Database Schema section 5 contradiction (event-sourced versus state-stored identity tables) was caught during pre-prompt reading for US-02.01 and resolved by treating the more specific document as authoritative. Had it not been caught, the prompt would have asked for an event-sourced aggregate against a non-existent event store.

The coordinator's `logger_test.go`-only under-specification on US-02.01 was caught by the executor, who extended the fix to `tenant_test.go`. The catch is in the executor column, but the root cause is a coordinator prompt that enumerated rather than instructed-to-search.

## Recommendations for Sprint 3

Continue the cadence unchanged: the two-step decision-then-prompt pattern, the branch-plus-PR flow, the PR-green-before-merge gate (without exception this time), the independent dependency-claim verification, and the executor report-back protocol. None of these needs modification.

Run the v1.1 reconciliation pass now, before or at the start of Sprint 3, as the Sprint 1 retrospective scheduled. The consolidated list above is the input. It is planning-side technical debt that does not block code, but it has grown to a size where a fresh session reading the stale planning documents as authoritative would be materially misled — the event-sourcing framing of identity in section 11 is the most dangerous instance, since it directly contradicts the implemented design.

US-02.03 is the natural first story of Sprint 3 by dependency order, and it is a heavy one (five points). It is where several deferred pieces all land at once: the regular RLS-bound connection pool with the `AfterAcquire`/`BeforeRelease` hooks that set `app.current_tenant_id`, the RLS policies on the identity tables, the `sessions` table, the login flow, and the session middleware. It is also where the user-enumeration timing defense deferred from US-02.02 belongs (verifying a wrong username against a dummy hash so response time does not reveal account existence). The coordinator should expect US-02.03 to need careful decomposition and possibly to be the story where `sqlc` is finally introduced for the first tenant-scoped queries.

The R-01 trigger is effectively cleared (cumulative 22 against a 21 threshold after only two sprints) but should still be formally re-checked at the end of Sprint 3 per the plan.

## Closing note

Sprint 2 delivered its committed scope cleanly: four story commits, zero carry-over, zero correction prompts, one merge-gate lapse with no consequence, one documentation contradiction resolved before it could become broken code, and a linear git history. The codebase at sprint close has a CI pipeline gating every change, structured logging and error conventions in force, a working tenant-and-owner bootstrap against a real BYPASSRLS role, and a complete password module. Sprint 3 can begin the session and login work after the v1.1 documentation pass. The work continues.
